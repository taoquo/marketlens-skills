# Event Taxonomy

Use this file to classify whether an event can change market perception or only creates noise.

## Event Types

| Event Type | Examples | Primary Thesis Channel |
|---|---|---|
| Earnings and guidance | Quarterly results, annual results, guidance, business update | Revenue, margin, cash flow, revisions, valuation multiple |
| Product and customer validation | Product launch, major order, design win, customer deployment, pricing change | Growth runway, market share, unit economics |
| Regulatory and policy | Approval, license, reimbursement, antitrust, export control, tariff, subsidy, policy meeting | Market access, margin cap, risk premium |
| Capital allocation | Buyback, dividend, special dividend, debt issuance, equity raise, M&A | Shareholder return, dilution, leverage, strategic direction |
| Ownership and supply | Lockup expiry, insider sale, major holder change, index inclusion/exclusion, block trade | Supply/demand, governance, liquidity |
| Legal and governance | Court ruling, settlement, investigation, inquiry letter, related-party transaction | Liability, governance discount, operating restriction |
| Industry and macro data | Monthly sales, prices, inventory, capacity, policy data, rates, FX, flows | Sector expectations, demand/supply, discount rate |
| Investor communication | Investor day, analyst day, conference, management change | Long-term target, credibility, narrative reset |

## Catalyst Strength

- Hard catalyst: dated event with direct financial, legal, regulatory, or capital impact.
- Soft catalyst: dated event that can shift expectations but needs follow-up evidence.
- Narrative catalyst: market story can move sentiment, but evidence is indirect or weak.
- Noise: event is known, immaterial, stale, or not linked to thesis variables.

## Materiality Tests

An event is material only if it can change at least one:

- Revenue growth, order/backlog, customer adoption, or market share.
- Margin, pricing, cost, utilization, or cash conversion.
- Capital allocation, dilution, leverage, or refinancing risk.
- Policy access, license, approval, reimbursement, or legal liability.
- Valuation anchor, terminal growth, discount rate, or risk premium.
- Thesis confidence, kill condition, or monitoring variable.

## Common Mistakes

- Treating every dated event as a catalyst.
- Treating a product announcement as adoption without revenue, customer, or usage evidence.
- Treating buybacks as bullish without checking valuation, cash flow, and dilution.
- Treating rumors as events without clearly labeling source quality.
- Ignoring that the event may already be fully priced in.
